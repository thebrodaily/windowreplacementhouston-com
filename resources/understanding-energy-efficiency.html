An Expert Guide

Window Energy Efficiency for Texas Homeowners

Not all energy-efficient windows are created equal. In Houston, blocking heat is far more important than retaining it. This guide explains what really matters on the label.

When you shop for new windows, you'll see an NFRC label with several ratings. While they're all important, the climate in Houston means you should pay special attention to one rating above all others. Understanding this will help you choose windows that genuinely lower your cooling bills and keep your home comfortable.

The Most Important Window Rating for Houston: SHGC

The Solar Heat Gain Coefficient (SHGC) measures how much of the sun's heat radiation is transmitted through a window. It's a number between 0 and 1.

Think of it like sunscreen for your house. A low SHGC is like a high SPF—it blocks more of the sun's heat. In a climate where we run our air conditioners most of the year, blocking solar heat is the single most effective way to reduce energy consumption.

For the Houston area, you should look for windows with an SHGC of 0.25 or lower. This ensures the window is rejecting at least 75% of the solar heat that hits it, which means less work for your AC unit and more money in your pocket.

The Second Key Rating: U-Factor

The U-Factor measures a window's insulating properties. It indicates how well the window prevents non-solar heat from transferring from the outside to the inside (or vice versa). Like SHGC, it's rated on a scale from 0 to 1.

A lower U-Factor means better insulation. While this is the most critical rating in cold climates (where you want to keep heat *in*), it's still very important in Houston for keeping the ambient summer heat out of your home.

For our climate, a U-Factor of 0.30 or lower is an excellent target that signifies a well-insulated window unit.

Houston's Rule of Thumb:

Prioritize a low SHGC first, then a low U-Factor.

A window with a fantastic U-Factor but a high SHGC will still allow solar heat to bake your home. The best windows for our climate excel at both, but SHGC is the undisputed champion for saving money in Texas.

The Technology Behind the Ratings

How do modern windows achieve these low ratings? It's a combination of advanced technologies working together:

  • Low-E (Low-Emissivity) Coatings: This is the workhorse of energy efficiency. A transparent, microscopic layer of silver is applied to the glass. This coating is spectrally selective, meaning it reflects the invisible heat-carrying infrared (IR) and ultraviolet (UV) rays while allowing visible light to pass through. This is how you get a low SHGC without making your home dark.
  • Argon Gas Fill: The space between the two (or three) panes of glass is filled with Argon, a dense, inert gas. Argon is a much poorer conductor of heat than air, which significantly improves the window's U-Factor by reducing thermal transfer.

What About the ENERGY STAR® Label?

The ENERGY STAR® program simplifies things by setting performance criteria based on climate zones. Houston is in the Southern Climate Zone. For a window to be ENERGY STAR certified in our zone, it must have:

  • A U-Factor of 0.40 or less
  • An SHGC of 0.25 or less

While this is a great baseline, we recommend choosing windows that exceed these minimum requirements (like a U-Factor of 0.30 or less) for the best possible performance and savings in our demanding climate.
